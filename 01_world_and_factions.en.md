# 01 · World & Factions

## I. The Country

### Name & Identity

**Official name**: La República Federal de Veladuria
**English**: The Federal Republic of Veladuria
**Common nickname**: *La Vela* ("the candle") — both because *vela* in Spanish means "candle" or "wake," and because outsiders joke that "this country's candle could be blown out at any moment."

> Etymology note: *Veladuria* derives from *velar* (to keep vigil, to hold a wake for the dead), with overtones of *vela* (candle, sail). A country holding a wake for itself while trying to set sail — a Marquezian double-metaphor.

### Geography & Population

- Located on a fictional Latin American landmass: bordering the Caribbean to the east (**El Mar Cordial**, "the friendly sea"), the *Cordillera del Toro Sangrante* ("Bleeding Bull Range") to the west. To the north, **sharing a land border** with a vastly more powerful neighbor, **El Norte** ("the Eagle"). To the south, **across a contested rainforest**, lies another regional power, **El Sur** ("the Bull"). **Veladuria is the buffer between these two great powers — and the side-stage where their rivalry plays out.**
- Population approx. **28 million**: largely mestizo, ~18% Indigenous (*los pueblos del Toro*), ~8% Afro-descendant (concentrated in coastal ports), ~4% European-descended colonial families (almost all of whom are oligarchs).
- Three core locations:
  - **Capital: San Cristóbal del Cobre** ("Saint Christopher of the Copper") — population ~6 million. Colonial old town + modern financial district + sprawling slums (*Las Veladas*, "the wake-keeping district").
  - **Coastal port: Puerto Sangre** ("Blood Port") — banana and copper exports, drug-smuggling hub, the gateway for every dirty deal.
  - **Highlands: Cumbre Mayor** ("Greater Summit") — old cattle ranches, mining belt, Indigenous communities, and the historical heartland of leftist guerrilla activity (now nearly extinguished).

### Economy & Currency

- **Currency**: el cobre (the "copper"). Originally pegged to the Northern currency; pegging was broken three years ago, triggering hyperinflation. Annual inflation ~280%. Black-market dollars (the *norteño*) are the de facto hard currency.
- **Pillar industries**:
  - Copper (40% of foreign exchange — but the mining bureau is corrupt and ownership is contested)
  - Bananas and coffee (monopolized by a handful of oligarch families)
  - Beef (the ranchers' bloc)
  - Remittances (sent monthly by undocumented immigrants in El Norte — the only thing keeping the bottom from collapsing)
- **Underground economy**: cocaine transit, counterfeit coppers, smuggled arms and electronics.
- **The treasury**: effectively bankrupt. The IMF has suspended lending, demanding structural reforms (cut subsidies, privatize state copper — copper being precisely what the oligarchs want their hands on).

**Key reference points**:
- **The 40,000 *norteños* deposit Vega offers in Sample 1 ≈ 80 of Saulito's street performances** — roughly two years of his income.
- Lucía has 4 terms left for her nursing degree, ~120,000 *cobres* ≈ 3,160 *norteños*. The 40,000 deposit covers her degree *and* her grandmother's bad knee.
- A "small gift" from the Espíritu Santo cartel typically runs 5,000 *norteños* in cash plus a leather case — **roughly three years of Saulito's income**. This is why "refusing" is a real choice, not just a moral one.

**Inflation as texture**:
Small details scattered through event text — "today's coffee is up another 5 *cobres*," "the bread line was three people longer this morning" — should accumulate into the atmosphere of a country in slow collapse. **Don't use percentages and GDP figures.** Use **eggs, coffee, the length of the line.**

### Recent History (the "national wounds" the player should know)

- **1973**: A leftist president, *Aurelio Mendoza*, pushes land reform and copper nationalization. **The military, with the tacit blessing of the Northern embassy, stages a coup.** Mendoza is "found suicided" in the presidential palace. Official textbooks call this "September Liberation"; the streets call it *La Noche Larga* ("The Long Night").
- **1973–1990 — military regime**: led by the *Castaño* family, a military dynasty. More than 14,000 disappeared. Leftist parties, unions, peasant organizations, and Indigenous activists are systematically eliminated. **This is why "the left" barely exists as an active faction in the present day — it was exterminated a generation ago.**
- **1990**: under international pressure and economic collapse, the regime "voluntarily" returns to civilian rule — on the condition that a *Reconciliation Act* immunizes prior political crimes from prosecution. The first elected president signs.
- **1990–2010**: a wobbly democratic period. Repeated economic crises, multiple presidents toppled.
- **2010**: **Augusto Báez Marín** wins on a "tough centrist" platform, promising to crush corruption, restore order, stabilize the currency. He actually delivers a few things — inflation back to single digits, murder rates halved, the country briefly hailed by international media as "the miracle."
- **2014, 2018**: re-elected twice. Term limits removed by constitutional amendment. The "miracle president" begins to look less miraculous.
- **2024–2026 (present)**: economic avalanche, drug cartels reasserting control, the Northern embassy meeting privately with opposition figures, junior military officers passing around a coup memorandum.
- **A certain night, 2026 (eleven days before game start)**: the President leaves the presidential palace, *La Casa Cobre* ("the Copper House"), in secret. The official statement: he is "vacationing in the south." No media outlet believes it.
- **The morning eleven days later**: you — a slum-dwelling street performer who makes pocket money impersonating the president at weddings and funerals — are taken away by an unmarked black Chevrolet. **The game begins.**

## II. Five Factions and the "Sixth Hand"

> The factions in this game aren't RPG-style "pick a side" choices. They're more like six vises pressing on the protagonist at once. **If any single one bottoms out, the ending arrives early.**

### Faction 1 · The Military (*La Junta*)

**Self-image**: The spine of the nation. From the wars of independence through the anti-communist era, there is no Veladuria without the army.
**How others see them**: The killers' sons. They want to kill again.

**Internal division**:
- **The Old Guard / Castaño faction (*Los Castaños*)**: led by Defense Minister **General Rómulo Castaño-Suárez** (72). Nephew of the original Castaño junta family. Nostalgic for the regime, bitter that the Northern embassy "betrayed" them. Pragmatic, corrupt, ostensibly loyal to the president — but only ostensibly.
- **The Young Guard / The New Ones (*Los Nuevos*)**: led by Presidential Guard Commander **Coronel Mateo Vega** (41). Born in the slums, worked his way through the academy. Sees himself as "clean." Privately circulates a coup draft titled *The 2026 Memorandum* — but he distrusts both the Old Guard *and* the Northern embassy. What he actually wants is "to get a truly clean person to run this country." **Mateo is the architect of the body-double plan** — he needs someone "who looks like the president, but isn't Báez and isn't Castaño," to buy time.

**Relationship to the body double**: You were put there by the Young Guard. But the palace you live in is full of Old Guard people.

**Typical event**: On day three of "your" presidency, the Defense Minister invites you on a private hunt and asks if you "remember this year's defense appropriations." He's testing whether your "memory loss" is real.

---

### Faction 2 · The Twelve Surnames (*Los Doce Apellidos*)

**Self-image**: The country's true owners. Not whatever president — *we* are the families who carved this land out of the jungle.
**How others see them**: The vampire syndicate the colony left behind.

**Core**: 12 colonial-era families that own the land, the copper mines, the banks, the TV stations, the banana and beef exports. Endlessly intermarried, but factionalized internally.

**Key figures**:
- **Doña Isabel del Carmen Ríos-Echeverría** (58) — current Minister of Agriculture, the informal spokesperson of the Twelve. Widowed, devoutly Catholic, attends Sunday Mass at *Catedral de la Aurora* without fail. Her voice is always gentle. When she takes your hand, she wears white gloves. She is the most dangerous of the oligarchs because **she truly believes she's doing the right thing.**
- **Don Federico Ríos-Echeverría** (Isabel's uncle, 68) — local representative for Northern Republic firm *Norhem Copper Co.*, and the leading candidate to benefit from copper privatization.

**What they want**:
- Privatize the copper mines (officially via IMF pressure, privately via their own shell companies).
- Delay or kill any land reform legislation.
- They don't care if the president falls — they only care that the government doesn't tilt left.

**Relationship to the body double**: Initially they think you're just the real Báez gone soft after a stroke. When they begin to suspect you're a double, their reaction won't be to expose you — it'll be to **absorb you.**

**Mariana's role through the story**:

- **End of Act II**: Isabel orchestrates a state dinner. Mariana appears for the first time. **Her tone toward you is unmistakably not the tone you'd take with the actual president** — she opens with a pointed test: "What have you been reading lately?" (The real Báez never read.)
- **Mid Act III**: She "happens to" run into Saulito on a private walk (this is no accident — she's been following you). She hands you an old book with a note tucked inside.
- **Act IV core conflict**: **Isabel proposes that the President divorce Gabriela and marry Mariana**, to consolidate the conservative coalition. Her stated reason: "Gabriela has gotten too populist these past few years; you deserve a partner who matches La Casa Cobre." This is the oligarchs' final move of absorption — once you (the body double) accept the engagement, **your private life is fully captured**, but your political capital becomes welded to the Twelve Surnames.
- **Multi-playthrough tension**: First-run players will mostly refuse ("I can't do this to Gabi"). Later runs will reveal: **Mariana herself doesn't want to marry "the President" either.** She's been pushed onto the stage by the same machinery that pushed you. This is one of the rare scenes in the game where two people, both stuck in roles they didn't choose, sit at the same table.
- **Possible end-state**: Mariana is one of the few people Saulito can be honest with — *"we are both performers."* But any genuine intimacy between them is politically toxic.

**Writing note**: Mariana's dialogue must always work on **two layers** — the surface political pleasantry, and an ironic subtext only she and the player understand. Example:
> "Mr. President, my mother sends her apologies — her cough is worse tonight. She couldn't make it."
> *Her gaze rests on your wristwatch for 0.7 seconds.*
> "Your father's Seiko is lovely."
> *Your father's Seiko. She said it too softly.*

#### Joaquín's Hit-and-Run — The Player's "Blackmail Card"

> Design intent: when the player faces oligarch pressure, they need **at least one card they can play back.** Joaquín's scandal is that card — but using it carries a price.

**The facts** (the player accumulates these gradually):
- One early morning in November 2024, Joaquín, drunk, hit a couple and the infant in their arms with his car on the outskirts of Puerto Sangre. The family fixed it: a sum of money, a lawyer, a forged "heart attack" doctor's signature.
- The police record was destroyed. But **at least three copies exist**:
  - The personal safe of *Suboficial Téllez*, a low-ranking officer who took the bribe and then disappeared (now dead; location unknown).
  - The hard drive of *Beatriz Solano*, a Puerto Sangre tabloid reporter (later forced out of her paper, now an independent investigator).
  - The Northern embassy's basement analysts (they never submitted it — they kept it as leverage).

**How the player obtains the card**:
- **Path A**: through Beatriz Solano's side-quest (Street & Church faction ≥ "tentative cooperation," plus a specific night-time event).
- **Path B**: via a "friendly suggestion" from Northern Ambassador Whitfield (Northern embassy ≥ "real cooperation," plus the player explicitly stating they "need leverage").
- **Path C**: through the Espíritu Santo cartel (they know everything — but their price is different; see below).

**What the card lets you do**:
- **Before a major Doña Isabel push** (e.g., the forced marriage proposal), playing the card forces her to back off (the marriage event is delayed by an act).
- **Force the Twelve Surnames to flip on a specific bill** (e.g., partially withdraw their support for copper privatization).
- **In the final act, as self-preservation** — leaking the scandal to the media to buy time when you're being unmasked.

**The cost of playing the card**:
- **Twelve Surnames *Sospecha* +30** — they immediately understand "this 'President' knows, and is willing to use it." They will **strike back, fast**.
- Hidden flag `USED_JOAQUIN_CARD` — every later oligarch event becomes one tier harder.
- **Moral cost** (Cordura −10): the couple really died. The infant really died. Playing the card means treating their deaths as currency.

**Special variant (Path C's hidden cost)**:
- If the card came from the Espíritu Santo cartel, **they don't ask for repayment up front.** But during a crisis in Act VI, they will "kindly remind" you "we helped you once."
- This is the first appearance of the game's "devil's contract" pattern: **the cartel never takes money — but they will collect at the worst possible moment.**

### Faction 3 · The Espíritu Santo Cartel (*El Cártel del Espíritu Santo*)

**Self-image**: Where the church can't reach, we do. Where the police won't go, we go. We are this land's true Holy Spirit.
**How others see them**: A killers' syndicate dressed in white.

**What's distinctive**: The cartel in this game is not a stock villain. They have built an "informal state":
- They have their own hospital (Espíritu Santo Clinic), their own welfare program (*El Ropero del Padrino*, "the Godfather's Closet"), even their own radio station (**Radio del Espíritu**) — playing music for the poor, broadcasting prayers to the Virgin, and laundering money along the way.
- In the slums of *Las Veladas*, the cartel has higher legitimacy than the government. When the President's police come to arrest someone, the neighborhood blasts sirens and children throw stones at the squad cars.

**Leader**: **Don Joaquín "El Padrino" Salazar** (55) — never leaves his mountain estate, *La Casa de los Mil Cristos* ("the House of a Thousand Crosses"). Sundays he distributes bread to orphanages. Tuesdays he holds meetings to order executions. He has never personally killed anyone. Nor has his daughter, *Renata*.

**Relationship to the body double**: On day ten of "your" presidency, the cartel sends a man in a white suit — not to threaten, but **to give**: a sum of cash, a piece of advice, and one phrase: "*Patrón, somos hermanos.*" ("Boss, we're brothers.")

**What they want**: For the government to leave their trafficking routes alone. In exchange, they can "help" the government with certain inconveniences — including specific oligarchs, specific foreigners.

### Faction 4 · The Two Great Powers (*El Águila y el Toro*)

**Self-image (North)**: We are friends of democracy, allies of Veladuria, coordinators with the IMF, the makers of stability.
**Self-image (South)**: We are brothers of the same continent. The Northerners don't understand this land — we do.
**How others see them**: Two large animals fighting in your living room. Every kick lands on your furniture.

> **Design principle**: The "great powers" in this game are **two**, not one. **Saulito's only path to survival is staying on the seam between them** — when either side's stance bottoms out, the ending arrives early. Each power treats the other as their primary adversary, with Veladuria as their nearest proxy battlefield.

---

#### 4a · The Northern Embassy (*La Embajada del Norte*) — "the Eagle"

**Official name**: Embassy of the Confederation of Northern States (*La Confederación de los Estados del Norte*) to Veladuria. Locals just call it *La Embajada* or *El Águila*.

**Defining tonality** (this is the key setting choice for El Norte):
**A liberal-universalist empire wrapped around a Cold-War-Soviet-style paranoia.** On the surface, they speak the language of democratic aid, market reform, human rights reports. Internally, **the place runs on intelligence-machine paranoia** — staff fill out "loyalty self-evaluations" on each other, expat officers undergo periodic psychological assessments, the potted plants in the hallways are suspected of being bugged (sometimes they are). **This is a paranoid empire wrapping itself in the rhetoric of liberty** — they sincerely believe they're helping Veladuria, and when Veladuria "doesn't accept the help," their reaction isn't reflection — it's **suspecting infiltration.**

**Key figures**:
- **Embajadora Helena Whitfield** (51) — Northern ambassador, former intelligence officer, fluent in four languages including a clean local-accented Spanish. She never threatens. She only **cares**. "Sir, we hear you've been having some… health concerns. We have some excellent doctors on our side…" She represents the embassy's "moderate" wing — believing in soft tools (relationships, economic leverage) over hard ones.
- **Special Attaché James Pierce** (38) — officially Commercial Attaché; in practice the embassy's "hawk." He is the contact point on the Coronel Mateo Vega channel, and he **genuinely** believes "we're helping this country." **He is more dangerous than Whitfield**, because he fears "losing Veladuria to the Southern Bull" more than he cares about Veladuria itself.
- **The embassy basement**: a team of unnamed analysts. They read every local newspaper, monitor every phone line, study Saulito's accent drift. They will be the first to **definitively know** you're a double. But their reports pass through Whitfield and Pierce, and *when* the report goes up — and to whom — is part of the embassy's internal politics.

**What they want** (multiple, conflicting goals):
- The State Department wants: stability, no more refugee waves, deny the Southern Bull a foothold.
- The Department of Energy wants: copper privatization, with Northern firms winning the bid.
- The drug-enforcement agency wants: action against the Espíritu Santo cartel.
- The counter-intelligence agency wants: to root out "Southern moles" — and the more they fail to find them, the more certain they become "they're here somewhere."

**Relationship to the body double**: The embassy has **suspected you're a double from day one**. They haven't moved on it. They want to see which way you tilt first. If you tilt toward the Southern Bull, the unmasking is **catastrophic.**

#### 4b · The Southern Embassy (*La Embajada del Sur*) — "the Bull"

**Official name**: Embassy of the Federative Republic of the South (*La República Federativa del Sur*; commonly abbreviated as *Vasconia*) to Veladuria. Locals call it *El Toro*.

> **About the name "Vasconia"**: in the early 19th century, during the Southern wars of independence, a generation of generals enamored with classical Europe named their new country after an ancient European people — the *Vascones* — for the imagery of being "free, untamed, never conquered." The name has been mocked by Vasconian intellectuals ever since ("why are we naming ourselves after a European tribe?"), but it has never been changed. **This perfectly captures Vasconia's internal contradiction: anti-colonial and anti-European on the surface; an elite culture that secretly worships Europe.** Its national anthem, the landmarks of its capital, its legal system — all carry the same off-kilter quality of "mocked for two centuries, but refusing to change."

**Defining tonality**:
**State capitalism + populist nationalism + a soft-power major.** Vasconia is the regional hegemon of the southern continent. It went through its own military regime; transitioned in the 1990s to "authoritarian democracy"; is now led by its charismatic president *Comandante Eulogio Vargas* (72, three terms in). Its economic model is state-owned oil + large-scale infrastructure + agricultural exports. It distrusts Northern finance bone-deep. It views Veladuria as part of its own sphere of influence, and its core diplomatic refrain is: "Why should the Northerners?"

**How it differs from the Northern embassy**:
- More **boisterous, more familiar** in style. Vasconian embassy staff drink with you until dawn at state dinners, give your son a signed soccer ball, call Saulito *Compadre*.
- **Softer instruments**: discounted oil, exported telenovelas, soccer leagues, cultural centers.
- **More populist paranoia**: they believe "the Northerners are running color revolutions everywhere," and they believe they have the right to "intervene in the name of anti-colonialism" by way of response.

**Key figures**:
- **Embajador Ramiro Aldana** (59) — Southern ambassador; former minister of sport (and former national team striker). Always smiling, sings off-key but with feeling, called by the local press "our favorite foreigner." Behind that smile is a continuously updated dossier titled *"How Veladuria's Elite Sees the South."*
- **Agregada Cultural Sofía Reinoso** (44) — officially Cultural Attaché; in practice the head of station for SIN, Vasconia's intelligence service, in Veladuria. She runs the South's influence network: TV stations, soccer clubs, several writers, one bishop (not Cardenal Soto).
- **Comandante Eulogio Vargas himself** (72) — President of Vasconia, three-term, **the only foreign head of state who interacts with the body double directly (at distance)**.
  - **Channels of contact** (he never visits in person — "the Veladurian climate is bad for my heart" — but reaches the body double through three independent channels):
    - **Video letters**: at least one per act. Long, "brotherly" monologues — soccer, family recipes, the IMF's "financial vampires." They wander; every meandering anecdote contains a concrete proposal. **The video letters are not skippable** — the player must watch them through, but can choose how to reply.
    - **Personal phone calls**: once each in Acts II, IV, and VI; always late at night. Vargas has been drinking. He becomes more direct, more reckless, more dangerous. These are the moments when the player is **most likely to hear the truth** — and most likely to be played.
    - **Public posts** (on Vasconia's own social platform *Voz*, parodied directly in-game): he posts 5–10 times daily; the content is chaotic. **Roughly 1 in 10 posts is a coded signal aimed at the body double alone** — an unexpected line from a 19th-century Veladurian poem, for instance, signaling a specific proposal.
  - **Personality**: the patriarchal, loud-voiced charisma of an old-school military man. He tells jokes, gossips about families, sounds completely uncalculating — but he is the second-shrewdest political player in the game (only Gabriela outranks him).
  - **Why he's dangerous**: **he genuinely likes Saulito.** He picked up "this president is different from the last one" from one of those late-night phone calls. The affection is real. But there is no buffer between his liking you and his deciding "I have to protect / use him."
  - **Dedicated mechanic**: the player's relationship with Vargas is tracked on a separate hidden axis (`VARGAS_PERSONAL`), invisible to the player. At a critical late-game moment — say, when the body double is being unmasked — a high `VARGAS_PERSONAL` triggers an event in which **Vargas personally calls and says, "I'm sending a helicopter."**

**What they want**:
- Pull Veladuria into the Vasconia-led "Southern Common Market" trade bloc (which means squeezing out Northern energy firms).
- Connect Veladuria's copper to Vasconia's smelting industry (**directly opposing Northern copper privatization**).
- Get Veladuria to "vote with the South" on the international stage — even one vote of margin would do.
- Keep the Northern drug-enforcement agency from setting up shop in Veladuria. The Espíritu Santo cartel is **not** Vasconia's ally, but Vasconia is quietly pleased to see it cause the Northerners trouble.

**Relationship to the body double**: The Southern embassy will figure out you're a double **later than the North does**, but once they figure it out, their reaction is **more proactive than the North's** — they will, **in the name of "protection,"** pull you onto their side, offering false-identity asylum, family protection, fallback plans. **This is Saulito's biggest "alternate exit" in the late game.**

#### Playing the middle: the player's "third-way diplomacy" between the two

**The core tension of the foreign-affairs line**:
- Both sides want you to pick. **Either side's complete victory accelerates the other's action.**
- The player can deliberately leak signals to both sides — "I might be tilting your way" — to extract critical resources (Northern loans / Southern oil discounts / coup intelligence from both). Each maneuver raises the *Sospecha* of the other side.
- Going to either extreme (full-North, full-South) triggers the other side's **devastating retaliation chain** (Northern divestment + media war; Southern border provocation + energy cutoff).
- **The truly hard game**: have both ambassadors appear at the same state dinner, both smiling at you simultaneously — survive that night, and you survive Act V.

### Faction 5 · The Street and the Church (*La Calle y la Iglesia*)

**Self-image**: *We* are this country. Not the people sitting in La Casa Cobre making speeches.
**How others see them**: The masses? They want bread; they'll cheer whoever puts up the bread.

This is not an organized faction — it is the umbrella of three forces: **public sentiment + media + the moral authority of the church.** Its "approval" measures whether your government is still alive on the streets.

**Key nodes**:
- **El Diario del Sol** (*The Sun*) — the mainstream paper, oligarch-controlled. Editorials gentle in tone, but always running stories that pierce you.
- **Radio Aurora** — the independent station; its director **Catalina Orozco** (37, former TV anchor — see also the body-double archetypes document). She is despised by the oligarchs, despised by the military, gently pushed out by the embassies — which is exactly why her listeners love her.
- **The TikTok generation** — a youth cohort nicknamed *Los Mocosos* ("the Brats"). They make memes about you and about the oligarchs in equal measure. They believe in nothing, but they show up to everything.
- **Cardenal Heriberto Soto** (74) — the country's most morally authoritative voice. He is most dangerous when he says nothing — once he so much as alludes to you in a Sunday homily, there will be people in the streets the next day.

**Relationship to the body double**: The street is the easiest crowd to win and the easiest to offend. A street performer with great instincts (Saulito's by-default reflexes) is, ironically, **better at handling the street than the actual president was** — this is one of the central ironies of the game.

### Secondary Group · *Los Pueblos del Toro* ("the Peoples of the Bull") — Indigenous Communities

> Design positioning: **not counted in the five-faction value system**, but appearing as the key NPC group for several important events.

**Identity**: Indigenous peoples of the *Cordillera del Toro Sangrante* region, comprising at least three linguistically distinct nations, collectively called *los pueblos del Toro* ("the peoples of the Bull"). Approximately 18% of the national population. Concentrated in the highland mining belt, the Cumbre Mayor plateau, and the southern rainforest fringe. **Their activists were systematically eliminated during the 1973–1990 military regime.** This broke their political organizing capacity; their **lifeways and community structures, however, survived.**

**Their core issues**:
- **The copper question**: ancestral lands are being eaten by oligarch mines. Any "copper privatization" simply locks the existing injustice in permanently.
- **Climate**: rainfall in the Cordillera has dropped sharply since 2024; traditional farming practices are collapsing.
- **Language and education**: the schools in Cumbre Mayor teach only Spanish; the indigenous languages are disappearing one generation at a time.
- **Recognition**: the constitution has **never** recognized them as "nations." Legally they are "citizens"; culturally they are "another kind of citizen."

**Key NPCs**:
- **Doña Aurora Mamani-Quispe** (68) — midwife and herbal healer in a Cumbre Mayor village. She is not a political leader (she rejects the title), but she is the de facto decision-maker on every major matter in the area. **She is the "fortune-telling old woman" the real President secretly visits monthly** — one of the most sensitive details in the President's private file. **In the first playthrough, Saulito will most likely be sent to the highlands "to maintain the tradition"** — his first meeting with Aurora. She sees through him at a glance, says nothing, and **cooks him a bowl of soup, listening as he eats.**
- **Joel Tupaq-Mamani** (28, Aurora's grandson) — anthropology student at the National University in the capital; on the edges of the *Mocosos* circle. One of the few people still trying to organize indigenous political power.
- **El Maestro Quispe** (55) — a mute carpenter / mystic. Sits at the back row of every Mass at *Catedral de la Aurora* — but he is not Catholic. He is there because it is the **only public space in the city where he is allowed to sit and not be turned away.**

**Relationships with the five major factions**:
- **Military**: deep historical grievances (1973–1990), but a small number of indigenous officers exist (mostly Young Guard).
- **Oligarchs**: outright opposition — the family ranches were built on their ancestral lands.
- **Espíritu Santo cartel**: complicated — the cartel has some highland presence (smuggling routes pass through), but local authorities like Aurora refuse to cooperate.
- **Northern embassy**: neutral — the North once funded a few "Indigenous development projects," none of which went anywhere.
- **Southern embassy**: ambiguous — Vasconia's "anti-colonial" rhetoric resonates, but Vasconia treats its own indigenous peoples no better.

**Role in the story**:
- They do not participate in the five-faction value game.
- But **1–2 critical events** (the fortune-teller visit, the highland miner occupation, the appearance of Joel at a youth protest) must pass through them.
- The player's relationship with them is tracked through **hidden flags rather than numerical values** — `AURORA_KNEW_AND_SAID_NOTHING`, `JOEL_FOUND_PRESS_HEARING`, `MAESTRO_QUISPE_NODDED` — none of which directly modify faction values, but all of which **change the tone of subsequent events and the unfolding of multi-playthrough revelations.**

**Writing principles**:
- Strict adherence to Document 06's Red Line 5: **they are not "noble savages."** They have internal disagreements, cynics, and compromisers.
- Aurora's prophecies have been right — and have been wrong. She is not a god.
- When they speak Spanish, **don't write pidgin.** Most of them are fluent; they just have an accent.
- When their language (a fictional indigenous tongue) appears, **transliterate only — do not translate, do not gloss.** Let the player feel the language barrier itself.

### "The Sixth Hand" · The Missing Real President (*El Presidente Ausente*)

Not a faction — a **suspense engine spanning all multi-playthrough cycles.**

- No one knows where Augusto Báez Marín actually is right now. The official "vacationing in the south" line is believed by no one.
- **Possible whereabouts** (revealed gradually across runs):
  - **A · A private hospital in El Norte**: under embassy "protection" — i.e., effectively detained. A Northern legislator's testimony at a certain hearing might leak the clue.
  - **B · An allied military base in the south**: he fled to a friendly base on the Vasconian side of the rainforest border, still has command intent, occasionally relays orders through a third-party messenger.
  - **C · Dead**: he died on some roadside the night he left; his body was disposed of in secret by Coronel Vega and a small handful of others. **Only Vega knows the full truth.**
  - **D · He never actually left**: he has been held captive within the country, in some basement or highland estate, by a faction (Old Guard military / oligarchs). And your existence as the body double is **the means by which the real President is kept from existing.**
- One of the long-term goals of the multi-playthrough player: assemble all the clues about him and **find him — or his body.** The end of this thread is the project's "true ending."

**Every faction believes itself to be the "real Veladuria."** This is the root of every irony in the game.

**The two-power rivalry is the table's "main diagonal"** — it repeatedly pulls every other faction into proxy struggle. The Twelve Surnames + the North are a stable alliance; Young Guard military + the North are an interest alliance; mid-rank military + the South are a cultural alliance; the Espíritu Santo cartel sides with no one — but cheers whenever anyone else has a bad day.
